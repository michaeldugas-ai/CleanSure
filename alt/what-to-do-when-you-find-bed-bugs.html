What to Do When You Find Bed Bugs

A step-by-step action plan for the first 24 hours to prevent spread and get professional help fast.

Don't Panic — But Act Fast

Discovering bed bugs in your home can feel overwhelming, but the most important thing to know is this: bed bugs are completely treatable. Thousands of households deal with this every year, and with the right response, the problem can be resolved quickly and thoroughly.

Early action is the single most important factor in preventing bed bugs from spreading to other rooms in your home. The sooner you respond, the more contained the infestation stays, and the easier it is to eliminate completely.

It is also critical to understand that bed bugs have absolutely nothing to do with cleanliness. They are hitchhikers that travel on luggage, clothing, furniture, and personal belongings. They are found in five-star hotels, college dorms, and suburban homes alike. There is no shame in finding them, only urgency in dealing with them.

The First 24 Hours

What you do in the first 24 hours after discovering bed bugs makes a significant difference in the outcome. The wrong reaction can turn a localized problem into a whole-home infestation. Here is what to do and what to avoid.

Do not move items between rooms. Bed bugs cling to fabrics, bags, and objects. Moving things from the affected room to other areas of your home is one of the fastest ways to spread the infestation. Keep everything in place until a professional can assess the situation.

Do not throw away your furniture. It may feel like the right instinct, but discarding mattresses, couches, or bed frames is almost never necessary. Professional heat treatment can eliminate bed bugs from all of these items without any need for replacement, saving you hundreds or even thousands of dollars.

Do not use store-bought sprays. Over-the-counter bed bug sprays are one of the biggest mistakes people make. These products rarely kill the entire population and, worse, they create pesticide resistance. The surviving bugs become harder to eliminate and may scatter to new hiding spots throughout your home.

Isolate the area. If the bed bugs are concentrated in one room, keep that room closed. Continue sleeping in your normal bed (we will explain why below), but minimize traffic and item movement in and out of the affected space.

Document Everything

Before you start cleaning or rearranging anything, take time to thoroughly document what you have found. This information is incredibly valuable for the pest control professional who will treat your home.

Take clear photos of any bugs you find, as well as any evidence like dark spots (fecal stains), shed skins, or tiny white eggs. Use your phone's flashlight for better visibility and zoom in on the details.

Note the exact locations where you found evidence. Write down which room, which piece of furniture, and which specific area of that furniture showed signs of activity.

Check the most common bed bug hiding spots carefully:

  • Mattress seams, piping, and tags on all sides
  • Behind and underneath the headboard
  • Joints and crevices of bed frames and furniture
  • Along baseboards near the bed
  • Inside nightstand drawers and behind picture frames
  • In the folds of curtains near the bed

The more detail you can provide to your exterminator, the more efficiently they can target the treatment and confirm the extent of the infestation.

What NOT to Do

When people panic about bed bugs, they often take actions that make the problem significantly worse. Here are the most common and most harmful mistakes to avoid.

Do not use bug bombs or foggers. Total-release foggers are completely ineffective against bed bugs. The chemicals do not penetrate into the cracks, seams, and crevices where bed bugs hide. Even worse, the pesticide residue can be dangerous to your family and pets, and the disturbance causes bed bugs to scatter deeper into walls and other rooms.

Do not use DIY heat guns or space heaters. Professional heat treatment works because it raises the entire room to a precise, sustained lethal temperature. Attempting this with consumer heat guns or space heaters does not achieve the required temperature uniformly and creates a serious fire hazard. Every year, homes are damaged or destroyed by people attempting DIY heat treatment for bed bugs.

Do not sleep in another room. This is counterintuitive, but sleeping somewhere else in your home is one of the worst things you can do. Bed bugs are attracted to the carbon dioxide you exhale and your body heat. If you leave the bedroom, they will follow you, spreading the infestation to your couch, spare bedroom, or wherever you relocate. Stay in your normal sleeping area until treatment day.

While Waiting for Treatment

Once you have scheduled your professional treatment, there are a few things you can do in the meantime to help contain the situation and prepare for the exterminator's visit.

Wash all bedding on high heat. Strip your sheets, pillowcases, blankets, and mattress pad and wash them in the hottest water setting your machine offers. Then dry them on the highest heat setting for at least 30 minutes. The sustained high temperature will kill any bed bugs and eggs on these items.

Reduce clutter near the bed. Bed bugs use clutter as hiding spots. Clear away stacks of clothes, books, magazines, and boxes from the area around and under your bed. This removes hiding places and gives the treatment better access to all surfaces.

Use mattress encasements if available. If you have or can quickly obtain bed bug-proof mattress and box spring encasements, install them. These trap any bugs inside the mattress and prevent new ones from establishing themselves there. They also make post-treatment monitoring easier.

Your exterminator may provide additional preparation instructions specific to the treatment method they will use. Follow those instructions carefully for the best possible results.
