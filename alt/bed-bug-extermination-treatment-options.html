Chemical Treatment

Chemical treatments use pesticides applied by licensed technicians to kill bed bugs on contact and leave residual chemicals to kill bugs that emerge later. While this has been the traditional approach for decades, it has significant limitations.

  • Multiple visits required: Typically 3-5+ treatments over 2-6 weeks
  • Effectiveness: 60-80% due to growing pesticide resistance
  • Toxic residue: Chemicals remain on surfaces after treatment
  • Cannot kill eggs: Most chemicals only kill live bugs, requiring repeated treatments as eggs hatch
  • Furniture disposal: Heavily infested items may need to be thrown away
  • Cost: $800-$2,000+ across multiple visits

Important: Bed bugs have developed resistance to many common pesticides. Studies show that some populations are nearly immune to pyrethroid-based products, the most widely used class of bed bug chemicals.

Fumigation

Fumigation involves sealing your entire home and filling it with toxic gas (typically sulfuryl fluoride) that penetrates all areas. While effective at killing bed bugs, fumigation comes with significant drawbacks.

  • You must leave home for 2-3 days while the gas dissipates
  • Highly toxic chemicals require professional handling and ventilation
  • Effective but expensive and far more disruptive than other options
  • No residual protection ‚Äî new infestations can occur immediately after
  • Food and medications must be removed or sealed

Fumigation is typically reserved for severe, widespread infestations where other methods have failed. For most residential bed bug problems, it is unnecessarily disruptive and expensive.

DIY Methods

We believe in being honest: most DIY methods are ineffective against established bed bug infestations. Here is what you should know about common approaches:

Store-Bought Sprays

Most over-the-counter bed bug sprays contain pyrethroids, to which many bed bug populations have developed strong resistance. These products may kill a few visible bugs but rarely eliminate an infestation. They can also drive bugs deeper into walls and furniture, making professional treatment harder later.

Diatomaceous Earth

Diatomaceous earth (DE) is a natural powder that damages bed bugs' exoskeletons, causing them to dehydrate. While it does work, it is extremely slow ‚Äî taking weeks to months ‚Äî and only kills bugs that walk directly through it. It cannot reach bugs hiding inside walls, electronics, or deep in furniture.

Steamers

Handheld steamers can kill bed bugs on contact, but their reach is very limited. You must make direct contact with every bug and egg, which is practically impossible given how well bed bugs hide. Steamers can be a useful supplement to professional treatment but are not a standalone solution.

Honest assessment: DIY methods typically cost $50-$200 in products but may extend your infestation for months. The longer bed bugs remain, the more they spread and the more expensive professional treatment becomes. Early professional intervention is almost always more cost-effective.
