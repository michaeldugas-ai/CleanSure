Travel Bed Bug Protection Guide

How to inspect hotel rooms, protect your luggage, and avoid bringing bed bugs home. Expert tips from professional exterminators.

Why Travelers Are at Risk

Travel is the single most common way bed bugs enter homes. Hotels, Airbnbs, hostels, and vacation rentals see a constant rotation of guests, and it only takes one infested visitor to leave behind bed bugs that will affect every guest who follows. The problem spans every price range and star rating, from budget motels to luxury resorts.

Bed bugs are expert hitchhikers. They crawl into luggage, tuck themselves into the folds of clothing, and hide in the seams of bags and backpacks. They are small, flat, and nocturnal, which means you are unlikely to notice them during your stay. Most people only realize they brought bed bugs home days or even weeks later, when bites appear or a visible population has established itself.

The good news is that with some simple precautions before, during, and after your trip, you can dramatically reduce your risk. This guide walks through everything you need to know to travel confidently and come home bug-free.

Before Your Trip

A small amount of preparation before you leave home can make a significant difference in your exposure risk during travel.

Use hard-shell luggage when possible. Hard-shell suitcases have fewer seams, folds, and fabric crevices for bed bugs to hide in compared to soft-sided bags. If bed bugs do come in contact with your luggage, they are easier to spot and remove from a smooth, hard surface. If you only have soft luggage, that is fine, but be especially diligent about the inspection steps when you return.

Pack your clothes in sealed plastic bags. Place your clothing in large zip-lock bags or sealed compression bags before putting them in your suitcase. This creates an additional barrier between your clothes and any bed bugs that might get into your luggage. It also makes the high-heat laundry process easier when you get home, since you can go straight from bag to washing machine.

Consider a bed bug spray for your luggage exterior. Several travel-sized bed bug repellent sprays are available that can be applied to the outside of your suitcase. While not a guarantee, these products can deter bed bugs from climbing onto your luggage in the first place. Look for products containing permethrin, which is commonly used on travel gear and clothing.

Hotel Room Inspection

When you arrive at your hotel room, Airbnb, or vacation rental, take five minutes to do a thorough inspection before unpacking. This simple routine can save you enormous hassle later. Here is exactly what to check and in what order.

Step 1: Leave Your Luggage Outside

Before you start inspecting, leave your suitcase in the hallway, in the bathroom (on the tile floor), or in the bathtub. Do not place it on the bed, carpet, or upholstered furniture until you have completed your check. The bathroom is the safest spot because bed bugs avoid smooth, non-fabric surfaces.

Step 2: Pull Back the Sheets

Start with the bed. Pull back the top sheet and comforter and examine the fitted sheet, paying close attention to the corners and edges. Look for small dark spots (fecal stains that look like ink dots), tiny rust-colored smears (blood spots from crushed bugs), shed skins (translucent, shell-like casings), or live bugs (small, flat, oval, reddish-brown insects about the size of an apple seed).

Step 3: Check the Mattress Seams

Lift the mattress edge and inspect the seams, piping, and tags on all four sides. These are the most common hiding spots. Use your phone's flashlight to illuminate the crevices. Bed bugs are flat enough to hide in the narrowest seams, so look carefully along every stitch line.

Step 4: Inspect the Headboard and Nightstand

If the headboard is attached to the wall, check along its bottom edge and the gap between the headboard and the wall. If it is freestanding, pull it away and check the back surface. Open nightstand drawers and check inside, along the drawer tracks, and behind the unit.

Step 5: Examine the Luggage Rack Area

Check the luggage rack itself, particularly the fabric straps. Inspect the wall area behind and around the rack. If the rack looks clean, this is where you should place your suitcase for the duration of your stay, keeping it elevated and away from the bed and walls.

Step 6: Check Upholstered Chairs and Couches

If the room has upholstered furniture, lift the seat cushions and check in the seams and crevices underneath. Check along the edges where the fabric meets the frame. These are secondary hiding spots that bed bugs may use if the population is large enough to have spread beyond the bed.

If you find any evidence of bed bugs, do not stay in the room. Request a different room that is not adjacent to or directly above or below the affected room. If the hotel cannot accommodate this or you are not satisfied with their response, consider changing hotels entirely.

During Your Stay

Even after a clean inspection, taking precautions throughout your stay minimizes risk. Bed bugs can be introduced at any time by housekeeping carts, neighboring rooms, or other guests.

Keep your luggage closed and elevated. Always use the luggage rack and keep your suitcase zipped when you are not actively accessing it. Never leave it open on the floor or on the bed. An open suitcase on carpet is essentially an invitation for bed bugs to climb in.

Hang clothes instead of using drawers. Use the closet hangers and hooks for your clothing rather than placing items in dresser drawers. Drawers in hotel furniture are common bed bug hiding spots, and placing your clean clothes inside puts them in direct contact with potential harborage areas.

Keep your belongings away from the bed and walls. Store shoes, bags, books, electronics, and other personal items on hard surfaces like desktops, countertops, or the luggage rack. Avoid placing anything on the floor next to the bed, on upholstered furniture, or leaning against the wall behind the headboard.

Coming Home Safely

The period between arriving home and fully unpacking is the most critical window for preventing bed bugs from entering your living space. Follow these steps carefully every time you return from a trip.

Inspect your luggage outside before bringing it in. If weather permits, do your initial luggage check in the garage, on the porch, or in the driveway. Open your suitcase and visually inspect the exterior seams, interior lining, pockets, and zippers. Look for any live bugs, dark spots, or shed skins. Even if you see nothing, proceed with the remaining precautions.

Wash ALL clothes immediately on high heat. Take every piece of clothing from your suitcase, including items you did not wear, and put them directly into the washing machine. Wash on the hottest setting the fabrics can tolerate, then dry on high heat for at least 30 minutes. The sustained high temperature is what kills bed bugs and their eggs. If you packed clothes in sealed bags as recommended, you can transfer them directly from the bag into the machine without exposure to your home.

Vacuum your suitcase thoroughly. Use a vacuum with a hose attachment to clean every surface of your suitcase, inside and out. Pay special attention to seams, corners, pockets, and zipper tracks. After vacuuming, immediately empty the vacuum canister or dispose of the bag in an outdoor trash container.

Store your luggage away from the bedroom. Once cleaned, store your suitcase in a garage, basement, or closet that is far from any sleeping area. Do not keep luggage in or under your bed, in your bedroom closet, or anywhere near where you sleep. This reduces the risk if any bed bugs survived your cleaning process.

If You Brought Them Home

Despite your best precautions, bed bugs can sometimes still make it home with you. Knowing the early warning signs and acting quickly makes all the difference between a minor issue and a major infestation.

Watch for these signs in the first one to two weeks after travel:

  • Small, itchy red welts on your skin, often in a line or cluster pattern, particularly on areas exposed during sleep like arms, shoulders, neck, and face
  • Tiny dark spots on your sheets or pillowcases (bed bug fecal matter)
  • Small blood smears on bedding from crushed bugs
  • A sweet, musty odor in the bedroom that was not there before
  • Tiny translucent skin casings on the mattress or near the bed

It is important to know that not everyone reacts to bed bug bites. Some people show no visible reaction at all, which means the absence of bites does not necessarily mean the absence of bed bugs. If you see any physical evidence on your bedding or furniture, take it seriously regardless of whether you have bites.

If you suspect you have brought bed bugs home, act immediately. Do not wait to see if the problem gets worse. Early-stage infestations are smaller, more contained, and easier to eliminate completely. Every day you wait, the population grows and the risk of spreading to other rooms increases.
